info Introduction

Beta lactam antibiotic, An acid-labile penicillin Penicillin V is an orally active member of the penicillin family. The discovery of penicillin by Fleming in 1928 and its exploitation by Florey and his colleagues a decade later is well known. The penicillin nucleus consists of a thiazolidine ring connected to a β-lactam ring to which is attached a side-chain. The side-chain determines most of the pharmacological and antibacterial properties of the penicillin in question. Penicillin kills bacteria by interfering in the synthesis of the bacterial cell wall

science Mechanism of Action

Penicillin kills bacteria by blocking the final step of cell wall synthesis. It binds to penicillin-binding proteins using its β-lactam ring, preventing proper cross-linking of peptidoglycans. This weakens the cell wall, allowing water to enter the cell uncontrollably, leading to cell lysis and death

assignment_turned_in Indications and Dose
Oral infections: Otitis media

Child 1–11 months: 62.5 mg four times a day, up to 12.5 mg/kg if necessary.

Child 1–5 years: 125 mg four times a day, up to 12.5 mg/kg if necessary.

Child 6–11 years: 250 mg four times a day, up to 12.5 mg/kg if necessary.

Child 12–17 years: 500 mg four times a day, up to 1 g if necessary.

Adult: 500 mg every 6 hours, up to 1 g if necessary.

Child 1–5 years: 125 mg every 6 hours for 10 days.

Child 6–11 years: 250 mg every 6 hours for 10 days.

Child 12–17 years: 250–500 mg every 6 hours for 10 days.

Adult: 250–500 mg every 6 hours for 10 days.

Prevention of pneumococcal infection in asplenia or sickle-cell disease

Child 1–11 months: 62.5 mg twice daily.

Child 1–5 years: 125 mg twice daily.

Child 6–17 years: 250 mg twice daily.

Adult: 500 mg twice daily.

Prevention of recurrent erysipelas (specialist use only)

Adult: 250 mg twice daily.

Acute sinusitis

Child 1–11 months: 62.5 mg four times a day for 5 days.

Child 1–5 years: 125 mg four times a day for 5 days.

Child 6–11 years: 250 mg four times a day for 5 days.

tips_and_updates Off-Label Uses
  • Prophylaxis against bacterial endocarditis in patients with congenital heart disease or acquired valvular disease undergoing dental/upper respiratory procedures
  • Moderate-to-severe gingivitis (with metronidazole)
  • Community-acquired pneumonia (CAP) in resource-limited settings caused by susceptible Streptococcus pneumoniae
⚠️ Note:
  • Off-label use should be guided by bacterial susceptibility, clinical judgment, and local protocols.
  • Not effective against beta-lactamase-producing organisms or many Gram-negative bacteria.

warning Important Safety Information

Advice to Patient

  • Take 1 hour before or 2 hours after meals.
  • Take at regular intervals, as scheduled.
  • Take missed dose as soon as remembered unless it's time for the next dose — do not double dose.
  • Inform your prescriber if diarrhea occurs, even 2+ months after completing penicillin therapy.
  • Contact your prescriber immediately if you experience:
    • Unhealed sores, chills, or fever
    • White plaques in the vagina or mouth
    • Fatigue, purulent vaginal discharge, itching
    • Hives, rash, or swelling of tongue/mouth/lips/throat
  • Complete the full course of therapy even if you feel better.
  • Stay well hydrated (2–3 liters per day), unless otherwise advised.
  • Patients on oral contraceptives should use additional contraception during therapy.

Superinfection

Prolonged use of penicillin may lead to overgrowth of non-susceptible organisms such as Candida or resistant bacteria.

If a superinfection occurs, appropriate treatment should be started immediately based on clinical assessment.

pregnant_woman Pregnancy Category: B
Penicillin V is considered safe during pregnancy and is commonly prescribed when clinically indicated. Animal studies have not shown fetal risk, though no adequate and well-controlled studies exist in pregnant women.
baby_changing_station Lactation
Trace amounts may be found in breast milk, but Penicillin V is generally considered appropriate for use during breastfeeding. Caution is advised if adverse effects are suspected or if infant sensitivity is a concern.
block Contraindications
  • Known hypersensitivity to penicillins
  • Previous antibiotic-associated colitis
  • Severe renal impairment
  • Infectious mononucleosis
  • Hypersensitivity to other beta-lactams

  • Seizures
  • Antibiotic associated-colitis
  • Leukopenia
  • Cholestatic jaundice
science Penicillin Resistance
  • Beta-Lactamase Production: Bacteria produce enzymes that break down the β-lactam ring, inactivating penicillins. These enzymes may be chromosomal or plasmid-borne. Gram-positive bacteria secrete them, while Gram-negative bacteria retain them in the periplasmic space.
  • Decreased Permeability to Drug: Resistance may occur due to reduced antibiotic entry caused by changes in the outer membrane or loss of porins (e.g., in Pseudomonas). Efflux pumps may also expel the drug, reducing its intracellular concentration.
  • Altered PBPs (Penicillin-Binding Proteins): Mutated PBPs have low affinity for β-lactam antibiotics, preventing effective binding. This is a major resistance mechanism in MRSA.
